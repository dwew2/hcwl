Corpws hanesyddol yr iaith Gymraeg 1500-1850: Cronicl Hywel ap Syr Mathew (chsm)

‘Cronicl Hywel ap Syr Mathew’, Peniarth ms. 168 (1568 (ms. 1589-90)), 198r-237r.

The text

The corpus contains a partial transcription of Hywel ap Syr Mathew's English Chronicle from Peniarth ms. 168 in the National Library of Wales. The chronicle was presumably composed in 1568. It bears no title in the manuscript. We here follow Prof. Thomas Jones who, in his edition of folios 227r.23-228v.23 of the text in Rhyddiaith Gymraeg i. 109-11, calls it ‘“Cronicl” Hywel ap Syr Mathew’. The Chronicle starts on folio 178r of the manuscript. The corpus transcription covers approximately the latter two thirds (folios 198r-237r) of the text, recounting events from the reign of William the Conqueror to the that of Mary I. It comprises approximately 21,000 words. The larger than usual amount of text given here is meant to compensate for the in parts rather formulaic nature of the Chronicle.

The source

The text in Peniarth 168 is a copy of the apparently no longer extant original made by Roger Morys of Coedytalwrn in the years 1589-90. A second copy of the text, mentioned in A Companion to the Literature of Wales (in the entry for 'Hywel ap Mathew') is unknown to us.

J. Gwenogvryn Evans (RMWL ... 960-2) makes the following remarks on Peniarth 168 and the Chronicle (rendition of dotted letters, on which see more below, is as in our transcription; mistakes in Evans's quotes have not been marked or corrected):

The author

HYWEL AP MATHEW (d. 1581), poet and historian, was a native of Llanfair Waterdine, Rads., where his family were landowners and where his father had been parish priest. One of the graduates of the first Caerwys Eisteddfod (1523), he was said by Lewys Dwnn to have been a bardic teacher. He addressed eulogies and elegies in the traditional manner to some of the leading figures of his time, including William Herbert, Earl of Pembroke, and Richard Davies, the Protestant bishop of St. David's. An accomplished scribe, he made copies of The Book of Arms and was a herald bard. His chronicle of British history, written in Welsh, which survives in two copies, follows the style of contemporary historians in England, beginning with the sons of Noah and continuing down to 1556. In it Hywel states that he had been present at the siege of Boulogne in 1544 and some of his comments suggest that he was a devout Catholic. Both Lewys Dwnn and Dafydd Benwyn wrote elegies for him. (The Oxford Companion to the Literature of Wales, ed. by Meic Stephens. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1986, 277)

The scribe

MORRIS, ROGER (fl. 1580-1607), copyist, of Coedytalwrn, Llanfair Dyffryn Clwyd, Denbs. The manuscripts in his hand which are extant, and which now belong to the Llanstephan, Mostyn and Peniarth collections, reveal the breadth of his interests: they include poetry, Lives of the Saints, heraldry, history, genealogy, romances, grammars and botany. He had access to manuscripts such as The Black Book of Carmarthen and The White Book of Rhydderch, and he copied parts of them. Some of his manuscripts were by 1607 in the possession of Thomas Evans (Thomas ab Ifan) of Hendreforfudd. (The Oxford Companion to the Literature of Wales, ed. by Meic Stephens. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1986, 414)

The language and orthography of the text

The following orthographic and linguistic peculiarites are noteworthy:

  • The respresentation of nasal mutation is rather diverse, and no basic 'standard format' is detectable. The following selection of examples of yn + stop, where N represents any nasal, and T represents any stop, illustrates the different spellings:
    • yn + voiced stop:
      • yN N- (that is, as in modern Welsh): yn + b-: ym mraint (209r.1), ym Manbri (219v.10-1);yn + d-: yn nechre (220v.27), yn niwedd (236v.21).
      • yN- (ie, single nasal, written as one word): yn + b-: ymordyr (217v.5), ymyw (220v.18), ymronn (229v.17); yn + g-: yngwystyl (206r.25), yngras (207r.31), yngolwc (215r.19).
      • yN T- (ie, yn assimilated, stop unchanged): yn + b: ym Brüttaen (222v.21); yn + g: yng Graphton (219r.13).
      • yn T- (that is, no sign of mutation): yn + b: yn Bossworth (221r.14-5); yn + g: yn Grinwits (221v.23).
    • yn + voiceless stop:
      • yNh- (ie, single nasal, written as one word): yn + p-: ymhümcant (213v.1), ymhob (226v.16); yn + t-: Ynhre (209r.27), Ynhy (236v.10); yn + c-: yngharchar (200r.21, 26, 201v.3-4, 204r.1), ynghalais (226v.5-6), ynghaer (200r.22, 204r.28 etc.), yngHent (231r.14).
      • yN- (ie, as above, but h omitted; rare): yn + p-: ??? ymlorhieth (217v.31-2); yn + c-: yngaer Wynt (199r.11, 199r.11), Yngressi (208*r.16).
      • yN h- (ie, single nasal, words separated before h-): yn + p-: ym Hlümwth (222r.6-7); yn + t-: yn hy (224v.17), ynn rhe (204v.2, 214r.5, etc.; hr- spelled as rh-); yn + c-: yng Hawnterbüri (212v.14), yng Hent (216v.27), yng Halais (217v.13).
      • yN T- (ie, yn assimilated, stop unchanged): yn + p-: ym Plymowth (212r.24), ym Pwmphred (218r.5); yn + t-: yn tre (212v.8, 212v.19, 217r.25), yn Tewksbri (220r.27); yn + c-: yng Cymrü (207r.2), yng Krakmor (223r.3), yng Calais (224r.9-10);
  • words with <ae> in modern Welsh are often spelled with <au>. Note the curious distribution of dauth vs. aeth, gwnaeth;
  • /aʊ/ (spelled <aw> in modern Welsh) almost always appears as <ow>, for example, anghyfiownder (200v.2), ydowsse (for adawsai) (203v.14), mowredd (208*v.32). The words llawer and llawen are generally exceptions to this;
  • /ə/ before /w/ (in nonfinal syllables) is commonly spelled <o> (for modern Welsh <y>), for example, kowirdeb (200v.14), vowyd (201v.23), ddowedud (202v.2);
  • pretonic weakening of /a/ to /ə/ (spelled <y>) is not uncommon, for example, gydewis, gydarnaf, and others;
  • <ph> is used for /f/ (modern Welsh <ff>) throughout;
  • in the third person singular past tense, numerous s-preterites (occasionally alongside -odd) and other formations (kymerth < kymryd, duc < dwyn) are found. Note that gwelas < gwel(e)d (never gweles; gwelas could be a southern form), dowod (< dywedud, cf. Middle Welsh dywawt), daeth(-) ~ dauth(-) < dyfod (the variant pret. stems doeth- and deuth- are extremely rare);
  • strangely, the feminine form trydedd is often used where the masculine trydydd would be expected;
  • there is a strong preponderance of the form mywn ‘in’ over mewn;
  • note the (presumably) past third person singular form gwelas and past impersonal gwelad (vs. gweles and gweled, which do not occur in this text; note that /a/ in these forms is unconnected with the dialectal development of /e/ to /a/ in final syllables, of which there is no trace in the text;
  • tydd- for tyf-, for example, tyddodd (210r.22, 215v.32);
  • the inflected stems of the prepositions gan and rhwng are ganth- and rhyngth- respectively, which together with other southern features above points to south-east Wales as the area of provenance for this text. This is broadly in line with the provenance of the text's author, Hywel ap Syr Mathew, who originates form Radnorshire. The copyist Roger Morris from Coedytalwrn in Denbighshire does not seem to have exerted any major dialectal influence on the text.

Details of the encoding

At one point two consecutive folios have inadvertently both been numbered 208. The second of these has been numbered 208* in the transcription files.

The scribe uses a number of letters with a diacritical subscript dot or diaeresis. These have been encoded as special character entities in the TEI-XML transcription files. As these characters cannot conveniently be displayed, ordinary letters have been chosen as standard replacements for them in the display files. The form of the manuscript can easily be recovered by reference to the TEI-XML transcription file. The following diacritic characters occur:

  • small <d> and capital <D> with subscript dot; encoded as &dd; and &Dd; respectively; equivalent to modern Welsh <dd> and <Dd>; standard replacement: <dd> and <Dd>; neither <dd> nor <Dd> occur in the manuscript
  • small <l> and capital <L> with subscript dot; encoded as &ld; and &Ld; respectively; equivalent to modern Welsh <ll> and <Ll>; standard replacement: <ll> and <Ll>; neither <ll> nor <Ll> occur in the manuscript.
  • small <u> with subscript dot; encoded as &ud;; equivalent to modern Welsh <w>; standard replacement: <w>; small <w> (and capital <W>) occasionally occur in foreign place and personal names (but are virtually completey absent in native words)
  • small <u> with diaeresis (umlaut); encoded as &uuml;; basically equivalent to modern Welsh <u>; as small plain <u> without diacritics occurs in foreign names and may occur for modern Welsh <f> word-internally, <ü> has been chosen as the standard replacement for <u> with diaeresis in order to keep the two distinct
  • small <v> and capital <v> with subscript dot (both rare and only used word-initially); encoded as &vd; and &Vd; respectively; equivalent to modern Welsh <w> and <W>; standard replacement: <w> and <W>. word-initially plain <v> is equivalent to both modern Welsh <f> (for which the manuscript also uses <f>) and modern Welsh <u>; word-internally <v> (besides <f>) represents modern Welsh <f> only.

The use of plain <u> as against <u> with subscript dot and <ü> in non-Welsh names is inconsistent; all of them occur, and there is some variation especially between plain <u> and <u> with subscript dot, compare, for instance, Edward (206r.11) and Eduard (206r.19).

Errors due to the inadvertent omission of a diacritic have usually been corrected and marked using a corr-element, except in the following cases:

  • non-Welsh names;
  • syntactic non-lenition (subject, object, genitive), where the text's usage may differ from that of modern Welsh;
  • non-dotted (that is, lenited) <l> or <L> following yn(n) ‘in’, which could represent the author's or scribe's usage.
